Executive Summary

The Wood River Women's Foundation uses a ranked-choice voting system where members rank their top 15 grant applicants from a pool of approximately 27 organizations. While familiar and straightforward, this approach has structural limitations that may not fully capture member preferences or ensure equitable outcomes across different grant sizes.

This analysis examines four voting approaches:

  1. Current Ranked System — Rank 1-15 from all applicants
  2. Participatory Budgeting — Allocate 100 points across applicants
  3. Quadratic Voting — Voice credits with escalating costs for strong preferences
  4. Tiered Allocation — Rank within grant-size categories

Key finding: Participatory budgeting offers the best balance of simplicity, expressiveness, and equal voice for WRWF's context. It makes tradeoffs visible, gives every member identical influence, and is intuitive for voters without requiring mathematical understanding.

The Current System and Its Limitations

How It Works

Members receive a list of ~27 grant applicants and rank their top 15. Points are assigned inversely: 1st place = 15 points, 2nd = 14, down to 15th place = 1 point. Points are summed across all voters to produce a final ranking.

Structural Problems

Problem 1: Rankings Don't Capture Intensity

A member who feels passionately that Organization A should receive funding and is indifferent about their 2nd-15th choices has the same influence as a member who carefully deliberated all 15 rankings. The system treats a "strong first choice" identically to a "weak first choice."

Problem 2: Large Requests Dominate

When $25,000 requests compete directly against $5,000 requests, larger organizations with broader name recognition tend to accumulate more points. A small but impactful program serving a niche population may be crowded out not because members oppose it, but because it's competing against flagship organizations for limited ranking slots.

Problem 3: Hidden Tradeoffs

Ranking doesn't make resource constraints visible. A member ranking 15 organizations at $20,000 each hasn't mentally allocated $300,000 — they've just listed preferences. The actual funding tradeoffs are invisible during the voting process and only become apparent when the board makes final decisions.

Problem 4: Strategic Voting Incentives

Members who understand the scoring system may strategically avoid ranking strong competitors to their preferred organizations. This undermines the goal of authentic preference aggregation.

The Equal Voice Question

While every member gets the same 15 rankings regardless of contribution level, the system doesn't ensure equal effective voice. Members who vote strategically or who happen to prefer consensus favorites have more impact on outcomes than members with minority preferences.

Alternative Voting Approaches

Participatory Budgeting (Recommended)

Each member receives 100 points to distribute across applicants however they choose. They can give all 100 to one organization, spread them across 20, or anything in between.

Key Advantages

Tradeoffs are explicit — every point given to Organization A is a point not available for Organization B. Members experience the resource constraint directly. Intensity of preference is captured: giving 40 points to one applicant signals much stronger support than giving 5 points to eight applicants.

Used by: Hundreds of cities worldwide for municipal budget allocation, including New York City, Paris, Madrid, and Porto Alegre (which pioneered the approach in 1989). The Participatory Budgeting Project has documented over 3,000 implementations.

Quadratic Voting

Members receive voice credits (e.g., 100) but the cost of expressing preference increases quadratically. One vote costs 1 credit, but 2 votes cost 4 credits total, 3 votes cost 9, and so on. This allows strong preferences while preventing any single member from dominating outcomes on multiple issues.

Advantage: Balances intensity with breadth. A member who cares deeply about one cause can express that, but at the cost of influence elsewhere.

Disadvantage: More complex to understand. The mathematical relationship isn't intuitive for all voters.

Used by: The Colorado House of Representatives for internal priority-setting; RadicalxChange foundation advocates for its use in collective decision-making; Taiwan's government has experimented with it for policy consultations.

Tiered Allocation

Applicants are grouped by grant size (e.g., Small: $5K-$10K, Medium: $10K-$17.5K, Large: $17.5K+). Members rank their top choices within each tier separately. Funding is allocated from each pool, ensuring diversity in grant sizes.

Advantage: Prevents large requests from crowding out small organizations. Ensures that small, focused programs compete against similar-scale requests.

Disadvantage: Doesn't capture cross-tier preferences. A member can't express that they'd rather fund three small programs than one large one.

Used by: Many community foundations and corporate giving programs use tiered approaches to ensure portfolio diversity. The Silicon Valley Community Foundation and Chicago Community Trust both employ category-based allocation.

Scenario Analysis

Scenario: Passionate Minority

Suppose 20% of members feel strongly that a small youth program deserves funding, while 80% are mildly positive about a large food bank.

  • Ranked: Food bank likely wins (more people rank it highly)
  • Participatory: Youth program receives concentrated points from passionate supporters; may compete effectively
  • Quadratic: Passionate supporters can spend heavily on youth program; outcome depends on credit distribution
  • Tiered: They don't compete directly (different tiers)

Scenario: Two Similar Organizations

Two food banks with overlapping missions both apply. Members who support food security might split their support.

  • Ranked: Vote-splitting hurts both; neither may rank highly
  • Participatory: Members can support both with partial allocations
  • Quadratic: Same as participatory
  • Tiered: If same tier, they compete directly within that pool

Recommendation

Participatory Budgeting with 100 Points

For WRWF's context — 245 members, ~27 applicants, a culture of engagement and deliberation — participatory budgeting offers the best balance of:

  • Simplicity: "You have 100 points, distribute them however you want" is immediately understandable
  • Expressiveness: Members can show how strongly they feel, not just their rank order
  • Equal voice: Every member gets exactly 100 points regardless of contribution level
  • Visible tradeoffs: The constraint is felt during voting, not just at the board level
  • Engagement: The act of allocating points is more deliberative than ranking

Implementation Considerations

The transition from ranked to participatory voting should be communicated as an enhancement, not a criticism of past practice. Key messages:

  • This approach gives members more ways to express their preferences
  • Your voice is now reflected in degree, not just order
  • The tradeoffs you experience are the same ones the board faces

A pilot year with clear feedback mechanisms would allow the foundation to assess whether participatory budgeting achieves its goals before committing permanently.
